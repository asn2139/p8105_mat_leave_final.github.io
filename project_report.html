Project Report

Questions

  1. How does length of maternity leave taken by women in New York City vary according to key socioeconomic characteristics?

  2. How does length of maternity leave differ by zip code? Do these spacial patterns match the distribution of key socioeconomic characteristics?

  3. Is the geographic distribution of average length of maternity leave associated with job type, co parent status, education, race, income, and borough?

Data

Our data was taken from the New York City Work and Family Leave Survey WFLS. It was conducted in March 2016 and was a telephone survey of New York City residents who gave birth in 2014. Its goal was to improve understanding about the availability and accessibility of paid family leave to working parents. The WFLS also sought to describe the role that paid family leave policies play in achieving health equity for parents and children. Additionally, it played a role in the passage of New York’s Paid Family Leave Law. A link to the Paid Family Leave Report can be found here.

We obtained the data from the NYC Open Data. From here, we exported the data as a csv file. The raw dataset contained 1063 rows, and 153 columns. We created new variables based on the questionnaire about variables of interest. Our new variable names described the question and the responses described the responses, rather than the numeric responses in the survey questionnaire. his data was then merged with zipcode shape files of New York City.

Our cleaned data consists of 1,063 rows and 20 columns. It includes information on the variables below.

Variables of interest*

Outcomes

  • leave_weeks = weeks of maternity leave (paid or unpaid)
  • paid_leave_weeks = weeks of paid maternity leave
  • unpaid_leave_weeks = weeks of unpaid maternity leave
  • postpartum_check = had a postpartum checkup 4-6 weeks after giving birth

Predictors

  • neighborhood = neighborhood (based on five digit zip code)
  • race = race of survey respondent
  • family_income = household family income
  • food_insecurity = concern about having enough food to feed family or receives public assistance in the form of SNAP benefits / food stamps
  • education = highest level of school completed
  • partner = co-parenting status (single or co-parent)
  • employment_type = type of employment during pregnancy (government, private company, non-profit, self-employed)

Spatial Analysis and Regression

We ran a Univariate Local Moran’s I test, looked for spatial clusters of high and low unpaid leave weeks. It looks at clusters in the same zipcode and surround zipcode as well. It also looks for places where there is low leave locally but high leave amongst the zip code’s neighbors, and vice versa. Since the Moran’s I statistic was 0.055 and had a p-value = 0.2131, we could not move forward with a spacial analysis since there were no significant spatial clusters.

After conducting this spatial analysis, we also ran a linear regression including a number of important covariates identified in our exploratory analysis. These covariates included job type, whether the parent had a partner, the number of weeks of leave they took total, the level of education they attained, and their race, income, and borough. The outcome was the number of weeks of unpaid leave they took.

Discussion

Our exploratory analysis did not find substantial differences of total or unpaid leave by race, parenting status, or income. This was not what we expected. However, one interesting finding from our exploratory analysis was that white mother and mothers with a four year college degree or higher were most likely to choose to stay at home. This highlights inequalities in maternity leave, as those who are more privileged in society were more likely to choose to stay out of the workforce. Given these findings, we wanted to better understand the role geography plays in type and length of maternity leave across sociodemographic characteristics. Overall, these maps suggest that type of maternity leave, length of unpaid leave, and sociodemographic characteristics are clustered geographically in New York City. This was more in line with what we were expecting

We performed a spacial analysis regression to formerly test these associations. The Moran’s I statistic was 0.055 and had a p-value of 0.2131, signifying that there were not significant spacial clusters. Based on these results, we could not move ahead with a spatial analysis. This was surprising and not what we expected.

Lastly, we ran a linear regression which included job type, co parenting status, total leave weeks, education, income, race, and borough.The only significant predictor of unpaid leave weeks was total paid leave weeks. This accounted for 64.35% of the relationship. This was also surprising, as we were expecting to see greater differences between sociodemographic groups.

As a whole, we were surprised with the lack of significant findings and spatial clusters. However, we think this may be attributed to the representation in the sample. 470 respondents were white while only 337 participants were non white. The way income categories were created may have also contributed to the lack of findings. The highest category for income was a family income of greater than 80,000. However, this may mask heterogeneity as the median family income in New York City in 63,799, according to the U.S. Census. Since the data was based on a phone questionnaire, it was almost exclusively categorical, masking heterogeneity. When attempting to build a predictor tool, we did not have enough data points to predict by neighborhood. Findings may have been different with a representative sample by geography and sociodemographic characteristics.

Lastly, an important limitaiton to note is that paternal and maternal leave are both important pieces in security economic stability within families and gender equality. We only had information on mothers. Future analyses should include a representative sample of New York City and include parents of all genders.
